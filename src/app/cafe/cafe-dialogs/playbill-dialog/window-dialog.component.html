THE YIDDISH THEATRE & THE DYBBUK

Jews wrote and performed plays in Yiddish throughout the world as a part of the Yiddish Theatre. The range was broad: opera, comedy, satire, melodrama, realism. The great-grandmother of the narrator acted in the Yiddish Theatre, performing at the Café Savoy in Prague, watched by Franz Kafka and Max Brod. She was a member of Yitzhak Lowy’s troupe, a man she adored, but who broke her heart. Lowy had an idea for a play he never finished writing, called The Broken Vow, and he told his idea to S. Ansky on a train in 1913; Ansky then stole his idea and wrote the most famous play of the Yiddish Theatre, The Dybbuk. The Dybbuk is considered a revolutionary play in both Yiddish and mainstream theatre. It’s been translated into many languages and is still performed regularly all over the world.

Our narrator, ____, finds a book in the library of Oblivion written by his friend, Józef, titled A Nearly Illustrated History of the Yiddish Theatre in Europe. In it are pictures and descriptions of actors and plays ___ has seen while traveling through Time in Oblivion, and then a blank page, with this caption:

From a production of The Broken Vow, Prague, 1919, Yitzhak Lowy’s troupe. Herschel Lowenstein as Chonen ben Nissn and Hanna Reimer as Leah.

____ says this is the “commemoration of an unwritten play performed by a non-existent cast.”

Dybbuks are spirits of dead people who possess the bodies of the living. In Oblivion, if one travels too much throughout Time, one can become a dybbuk, which is even worse than being a customer in the Café of Minor Authors.
